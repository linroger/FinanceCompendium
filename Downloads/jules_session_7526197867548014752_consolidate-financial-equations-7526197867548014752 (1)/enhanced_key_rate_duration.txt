**Mathematical Derivation or Foundation**
Key rate duration decomposes total bond price sensitivity into sensitivities at specific maturity points (key rates) on the yield curve. Mathematically, it's the partial derivative KRD_k = -(1/P) ∂P/∂y_k, measuring price change from a localized 1bp shift at maturity k while holding other maturities constant. The total duration equals the sum of all key rate durations: D_total = Σ KRD_k. This local perturbation approach captures non-parallel yield curve movements that standard duration misses. The formula comes from first-order Taylor expansion of the bond pricing function around the current yield curve.

**Key Assumptions and Limitations**
Assumes additive effects of key rate shifts, ignoring correlations between different maturities. Requires small rate changes for derivative approximation validity. Assumes yield curve can be adequately represented by discrete key rates (typically 6-12 points). Local perturbations may not capture systemic yield curve shifts. Ignores volatility smile or term structure of volatility. Breaks down for bonds with embedded options where effective duration is needed. Market liquidity constraints may prevent actual implementation of key rate trades.

**Practical Applications in Finance**
Enables precise hedging of yield curve risk by targeting specific maturity segments. Used in portfolio immunization strategies to match duration profiles. Critical for risk attribution analysis in fixed income portfolios. Applied in central bank operations for managing yield curve shape. Essential for mortgage portfolio hedging against prepayment-driven yield curve changes. Used in relative value trading strategies exploiting yield curve mispricings.

**Related Concepts and Extensions**
Extends standard duration to multi-point sensitivity analysis. Connects to principal component analysis for identifying systematic yield curve factors. Related to key rate convexity for second-order effects. Generalizes to key rate Vega for volatility sensitivity. Links to scenario analysis using parallel and non-parallel yield curve shifts. Forms foundation for multi-asset risk management frameworks.

Matrix operations become computationally intensive for large portfolios. Real-time risk systems need efficient caching and incremental recalculation methods.
