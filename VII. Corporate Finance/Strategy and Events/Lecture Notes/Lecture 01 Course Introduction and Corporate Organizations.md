# Assets and Investment Decisions

Real Assets

○ Physical – land, plant and equipment, computer hardware  
$\circ$  Intellectual - software, patents, knowhow

- Financial Asset

○ A financial claim, including unrestricted ownership, on real assets, or on other financial assets

Securities are a subset of financial assets

- Not all financial assets are securities  
- Despite what the text says!

# Assets and Investment Decisions

- Investment Decision

Purchase decision regarding real or financial assets  

- Disinvestment or exit decision – sale of some all of an investment

Financing Decision

o Sale of financial assets to fund an investment decision

Securities e.g., stocks or bonds  
Loans

# Assets and Investment Decisions

- Capital Budget/Capital Expenditure

○ Amount to be spent for investment purposes in the year  
○ Separate from Operating Budget/Operating Expenses

- Capital Structure

$\circ$  Set of liabilities which fund an investment  

- Usually used in reference to a corporation or trust

# Business Organizations

- Many forms of business organization  
- Cost and ability to obtain those forms may vary widely  
- Focus on corporate form of organization
- Widely available and relatively inexpensive to create in most developed countries

# Business Organizations

- Organization types available in the United States
- Sole Proprietorships  
$\circ$  Partnerships  
Limited Liability Corporations  
Subchapter C and S Corporations  
$\circ$  Not-for-Profit Corporations  
- Property rights and liability limitations vary

# Sole Proprietorships

- Typically small businesses  
- Example: local retail shop  
- Owned by a single individual

$\mathrm{O}$  Few employees

- Owner has unlimited liability  
○ Personally liable for liabilities of business  
- Lenders often require collateral for new or small businesses

# General Partnerships

- General Partnerships allow multiple individuals or other entities to own a business

Share profits and losses pro rata  
Share unlimited liability individually for partnership's risks  

- Ends with death or withdrawal of a partner
- Each partner fully liable for all other partners' decisions

○ Each partner may bind firm – jointly and severally liable

# General and Limited Partnerships

- General Partner

○ Managing Partner  

- Controls decisions of the partnership

Unlimited liability  

- May be a corporation, often a limited liability corporation (LLC)
- LLC as general partner

○ Provides limited personal liability to each individual GP

# General and Limited Partnerships

- Two types of partnerships

○ General Partnerships  
Limited Partnerships

- Limited partnerships

○ Liability limited to capital invested  
○ Limited or no role in activities of partnership  
○ Death or withdrawal does not dissolve partnership

# Limited Liability Corporations (LLCs)

- Incorporated within a given state of the United States  
Typically Delaware

○ Long history of corporate legal decisions  
O Viewed as fair or favorable to corporations

- May be incorporated in any US state

# Limited Liability Corporations (LLCs)

- Size of LLCs may range from an individual who owns an asset (say a farm) to companies with $ 1 billion or more of sales

○ Often family owned companies or otherwise closely-held  
○ Many owners involved as management or directors

- Taxed as partnerships

$\circ$  Profits and losses pass through to owners pro rata

# Limited Liability Corporations (LLCs)

- Used frequently by private equity firms as investment vehicles
- Advantages of partnership taxation structure  
$\circ$  Still maintain limited liability  
Principal Disadvantage:

- Convert LLC to a C corporation if company goes public  
Alternative to create C corporation holding company

# Type C Corporation

- Named after Subchapter C of the US Internal Revenue Code  
- C corporations are taxed as a separate entity from owners  
- Most large corporations are C corporations  
- Much more flexibility to raise capital – "take public"

○ Issue shares – interests in the company  
○ May issue dividends

# Type C Corporation

- When taken public, known as an Initial Public Offering (IPO)  
- May be purchased by any type of investor

○ Individual  
$\circ$  Mutual or pension fund  
○ Foreign investor

- May be private

# Type C Corporation Advantages

- Distinct legal entity with rights similar to an individual in some respects – "legal person"  
- Ease in ownership transfer (purchase and sales of shares)

Low transaction costs  
○ Corporations have "unlimited" life  
$\circ$  Limited liability for shareholders  
○ Increased regulatory scrutiny, if public

# Not-for-Profit Corporations<sup>1</sup>

- US and many other countries allow certain types of corporations to not pay corporate taxes

$\circ$  Charitable, Religious, and Educational Organizations (501(c)(3))  
○ Social Welfare Organizations (501(c)(4))  
○ Other Nonprofit or Tax-exempt Organizations (501(a-k))

# Not-for-Profit Requirements

- Principal reason for organization is not to make a profit  
- Corporate earnings may not go to any owner

○ May earn a salary and bonus

- Need to apply to Internal Revenue Service  
- Continuing reporting requirements

# B Corporations²

- Not a federal designation – no tax advantages  
Certified by B Lab a nonprofit

$\mathrm{O}$  Purpose:

■ Demonstrate commitment to stakeholders broadly  
Examples: environment, local community, employees

Currently over 5000 globally  

- Governance must be incorporated in charter

# Maximizing Shareholder Value

- Free market capitalism  
- Friedman doctrine on business and social responsibility
- Maximize the company's value  
$\circ$  Return money to investors (your employers)  
○ "Well-functioning" financial markets

# Maximizing Shareholder Value

Shareholders have diverse goals – impossible to fully satisfy all

○ Young/Old  
$\bigcirc$  Risk Tolerance  
○ Environmental, Social, Governance

- Investors need to account for above – not company management  
- By making investors as rich as possible, management provides maximum freedom of choice

# Maximizing Shareholder Value

# - Assumptions

○ Managers able to maximize Net Present Value of firm  
○ All companies play by the same rules

Taxes  
Labor laws  
■ Environment, safety and other regulations

$\circ$  Concentrated versus diversified interests behave similarly
