$$

# Modern Portfolio Theory (1952)

- Harry Markowitz postulated as follows, if financial assets have the following price distribution:  
$\circ$  Returns of financial asset  $i, r_{i},$  are normally distributed  
$\circ$  Each  $r_i$  has a standard deviation,  $\sigma_i$ , which is constant and  
$\circ$  A covariance,  $\sigma_{ij}$ , between all other financial assets,  $j$ , also assumed constant  
- If above assumptions were above correct and transaction costs were zero:

# Modern Portfolio Theory (1952)

- A portfolio of such assets has the following properties:

$\circ$  Expected return,  $r_p$ , equals the sum of the proportion of each asset's return  
$\bigcirc$  Portfolio variance,  $\sigma_{p}^{2}$ , a determined by the variance of each asset's return and that return's with all other assets

- If all financial assets had these properties:

One could construct a minimum variance portfolio  

- At any desired risk level

# Diversification and Risk Reduction

- Markowitz's insights led to research demonstrating that despite some of the assumptions being violated:

Diversification substantially reduced risk without reducing return  
o As few as 30 securities track the overall market closely

# General Formula for Portfolio Diversification

- General formula for computing variance of a portfolio is:

$$
V a r i a n c e = \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {N} \sum_ {j = 1} ^ {N} x _ {i} x _ {j} \sigma_ {i j},
$$

the sum of the weights of each security times their variance, and their covariance with every other security in the portfolio

- Typically not calculated precisely for all available stocks  
- Investors buy index funds

○"Smart" beta funds

# Contribution of Risk

- The market risk of the securities in a portfolio determines the degree of portfolio diversification  
- Standard measure of market risk,  $\beta$ , equal 1 for "market"

$\circ$  Investments with:

- $0 < \beta < 1$  move same direction but less than market  
- $1 < \beta$  move same direction but more than market  
- $\beta < 0$  move in the opposite direction of the market

$\circ$  Example: JP Morgan,  $\beta = 1.14$

- On average, expect JPM increase  $11.4\%$  if stock market increases  $10\%$ .

# Specific Risk and Market Risk

- Investors are not compensated for diversifiable risks  
- Market risk is non-diversifiable so is compensated

# Contribution of Risk

Definition of  $\beta$  of a stock or portfolio:

$$
\beta = \sigma_ {i m} / \sigma_ {m} ^ {2}, t h a t i s,
$$

the covariance of stock  $i$  with the market divided by the market variance

- Market risk accounts for most of the risk of a well-diversified portfolio

$\circ$  Average  $\beta$  of stocks in the portfolio determine its riskiness
