# Modigliani and Miller Debt Theorems

- Grew out of payout policy debate  
- In the 1950s finance community divided into two camps:

○ Companies achieve higher valuations if pay high dividends  
○ Companies achieve higher valuations if pay little or no dividends and reinvest in company

- In 1961 Miller and Modigliani show that dividend policy does not matter  
- All that matters is the market value of the company's assets

# Modigliani and Miller Debt Theorems

- As we have discussed the market value of the company's assets must equal the present value of its future cash flows  
- The Capital Structure of a company is its chosen mix of debt, equity and other liabilities
- Financial Leverage (gearing) refers to the relative amount of debt to equity a company employs  
○ If a firm's value is based solely on the value of its assets then capital structure should not matter – why are investment bankers paid so much?

# Financial Risk and Expected Return

- Recall, Harry Markowitz's modern portfolio theory

$\circ$  Applying it to the securities of a single firm, we obtain:

$$
r _ {A} = \left(\frac {D}{D + E}\right) \times r _ {D} + \left(\frac {E}{D + E}\right) \times r _ {E}, i n w o r d s,
$$

the proportion of debt  $\times$  expected return on debt + the proportion of equity  $\times$  expected return on equity

- This is the company's weighted cost of capital (WACC)

# Financial Risk and Expected Return

- To find the return on levered equity we solve for  $r_E$

$$
r _ {E} = r _ {A} + (r _ {A} - r _ {D}) \times \left(\frac {D}{E}\right)
$$

- This leads to MM II:

The expected rate of return on the common stock of a company is proportional to its debt/equity ratio  
$\circ$  Note:  $r_{E} = r_{A}$  for unlevered companies
