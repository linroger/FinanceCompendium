# Positive On The Business, Cautious On The Stock, Netflix Sale Is Unlikely

Netflix has a long runway for growth due to its first mover advantage and self-reinforcing model. Increasing content and user experience (UX) investment drives subscriber growth and pricing power, which funds further content and UX investment. However, we see the risk/reward on the stock as unattractive. This is a very long duration, high multiple investment with market expectations that appear too high through 2020, when most analysts seem to be looking for valuation support. We are skeptical that Netflix will be acquired.

# Consensus Estimates Reflect An Optimistic Case With Downside Risk

Our EBIT estimates are lower than consensus every year through 2020, with higher expenses as the driver of the difference in 2017, and lower revenue increasingly the primary driver in 2018-2020. Our 2017-2020 cumulative EBIT forecast is  $27\%$  lower than consensus, while our EPS is  $32\%$  lower.

Netflix's Business Is Too Fully Formed/Valued To Be Acquired By Disney/Apple Lack of a compelling strategic rationale and severe economic/earnings dilution are major obstacles to a potential combination.  $25\%$  EPS Dilution for Disney.

Our Forecast Reflects A Very Successful Business Model Over The Long Term We forecast 200M subscribers in 2026 (83M today),  \$27B in revenue ($ 9B in 2016),  $8B in EBIT (\$ 0.2B in 2016), and 28% EBIT margin. This would put Netflix at the same size (based on EBIT) as TWX today. If the market values Netflix in 2026 at the same EBIT multiple as TWX today, it would imply a ~7% rate of return on NFLX's current stock price. While not the valuation methodology we use to derive our PT, we think it helps contextualize valuation.

# We Do Not Think Access To Content Is Becoming A Problem For Netflix

Netflix's pivot to original programming, the development of its own in-house studio, the growth in aggregate studio output, and the size of Netflix's programming budget all mitigate the apparent risk from Amazon, Hulu, and local international players increasing their SVOD programming spend.

# Is Competition An Increasing Risk? Netflix Is Winning So Far, Amazon Is A Risk

Netflix has become the must-have SVOD service in the US, it is a complement to pay TV for the vast majority of users, and internationally it has had success in markets where local TV incumbents have failed. Consequently, we think Netflix will continue to succeed given its constant re-investment into content and UX. Yet, we think Amazon is the key competitor to watch.

# Domestic Subscriber Growth Deceleration Underscores Need To Re-Invest

With penetration exceeding  $50\%$  of US broadband homes, the incremental subscriber is more difficult to attract. We see the incorporation of Netflix into cable and DBS STBs, as well as continued content and UX spend as drivers of continued subscriber growth, albeit at a decelerating rate.

# Valuation

We use our economic returns model, a market-based DCF, to forecast future equity value which we then discount back, using our estimated  $12.5\%$  cost of equity, to a 1-year forward target. In Netflix's case, we've used an extended forecast period (through 2026). We begin by capitalizing the growth in unlevered free cash flow at the WACC  $(12.5\%)$ , adding the value creation to enterprise value. We then model the capital structure, including potential share count reduction through share repurchases, to derive a future per share equity value.

# Risks

Risks (to the upside) include: (1) Lower programming and other expenses than we've forecasted, (2) Greater subscriber and pricing growth than we've forecasted, particularly in international markets where the range of outcomes is much wider, (3) Successful entry into China, and (4) Netflix being acquired.

# Competitive Positioning

# We See Netflix As A Secular Winner

# One of the Few Companies in Media Where Fundamentals Are Improving

Netflix continues to effectively execute a successful strategy in the US, offering a better viewing experience than traditional Television, while also continuing its international expansion. Netflix's differentiated customer experience is rooted in its absence of advertising, the ability to binge watch entire seasons of shows, a simple user interface (UI) that facilitates content discovery through both search and recommendation, a near ubiquitous device/platform footprint, and a constant focus on continuously improving the user experience (including the content, user interface, network performance, and video resolution).

# Subscribers, Usage per Subscriber, and Domestic ARPU All Continue To Grow

The proof of the product's acceptance by the customer is obvious when looking at the KPIs over the last several years. Domestic subscriber growth remains impressive despite high penetration, and the pace of international growth is torrid. FX headwinds internationally and deliberate patience on pricing has kept international ARPU growth modest, but supportive of further subscriber growth. Subscriber engagement, as measured by hours streamed per day, has also grown nicely.

# Netflix's Business Model Is Self-Reinforcing

One can only admire Netflix's strategic gamesmanship in licensing syndicated content from US TV incumbents in order to build its user base to a point at which it had enough scale to begin commissioning its own original content; a move that was essential to establishing a sustainable competitive position. The continuous investment in expanding the available content, particularly original content, leads to growth in viewing per subscriber (driving pricing power and improved retention) and growth in subscribers. International expansion further supports the establishment of Netflix as a scaled player, allowing for increased originals investment with the international segment bearing a significant portion of the cost.

Scale vis-a-vis a large subscriber base, exclusive content and information asymmetry are Netflix's competitive advantages. Netflix can take more creative risk, has better information than the sellers of a show regarding a show's value, and it knows more about each subscriber than other aggregators due to the time spent on the platform. Furthermore, Netflix has a culture and focus on being great at what they do, but nothing else. A culture of continuous improvement has been the driving force behind the company.

# But, We Don't See SVOD As A Winner-Take-All Market

# Is Netflix An Aggregator (Network) or Platform (Distributor)? Does it matter?

In the historical Pay-TV context, Netflix is an aggregator, without question, since it and it alone decides what programs to procure/create and make available as part of its service. Furthermore, Netflix is increasingly being offered through other platforms alongside other aggregators. Such platforms include traditional Pay-TV operator STBs and internet platforms such as Amazon, Roku, and Apple TV.

Netflix is also a distributor in one sense because it has direct relationships with all of its subscribers, but it isn't a distributor in another (historical Pay-TV) sense because other aggregators are not available on Netflix.

Some argue that Netflix is becoming a platform (like cable TV) and this is important because it means people are going to Netflix to "see what's on" as opposed to going to NFLX for a given show. While there's some truth to this statement, particularly in certain market segments, we don't see this as a bright line between NFLX and other aggregators (i.e. broadcast and cable networks) in this regard. People do go to CBS, Discovery Channel, ESPN, and HGTV (to name a few) linear channels and their on-demand menus to find something to watch. Does that mean that all of these aggregators are platforms? We don't think so. It means they are all branded aggregators. Strong content feeds aggregator brands and strong brands drive viewers to content, creating a virtuous circle - like we're seeing with Netflix. We do, however, see a grey area in this regard, where Netflix is more of a platform than these other aggregators because its greater scale in content gives users reason to spend more time viewing Netflix relative to other aggregators; and Netflix does have its own user interface that fosters greater viewer captivity.

# Fragmentation Is Both Friend And Foe To Netflix

The well organized historical model of distributors paying aggregators for carriage and then organizing the content into channels and menus through their UIs is changing. In the past, consumers went to a central place for

content search and discovery. Consumers are now going to multiple places for content search, discovery, and consumption. While we believe consumers would still prefer a central interface for all TV search and discovery, it doesn't exist and the desire for specific content and different forms of viewing (on-demand, ad-free, consumable on any screen) are overcoming the desire for centralization.

Two important conclusions can be drawn from the existence of a diversity of consumer tastes, a very abundant supply of high quality programming, and an inability for any single company to capture sufficient industry revenue to corner the market for content. First, we think it means that Netflix should continue to grow its business and have a sustainable position in the market. Second, we think it means that this is not a winner-take-all market.

# Competition From Well Capitalized Players Is Intensifying

Over the past two years, we've seen a reinvigoration of Hulu's SVOD service with increased programming investment, an aggressive ramp up in content investment by Amazon, more full season stacking rights secured by traditional TV networks, the re-launch and expansion of live streaming bundles from Sony and DISH, the launch of direct-to-consumer offerings from traditional networks, the announcement of new streamed pay TV services to be introduced by Hulu and DIRECTV over the next several months, and Google's YouTube Red service beginning to make some noise.

# Consumers Want Choice & Breadth

We think the vast majority of consumers will continue to want a mix of live and on-demand content, and will source content from multiple aggregators and platforms. Content companies will be judicious about how and when they window their content. As a result, we think a bundle (maybe not "The Bundle" in all cases) will continue to exist in some form. Traditional distributors, therefore, continue to have an opportunity to add value. New distributors, the likes of Amazon Firestick, Apple, Roku, etc. also have an opportunity to create platforms that integrate the best of TV content. Platforms like Comcast's X1 or Charter's Spectrum Guide also have an opportunity to host 'new aggregators' like Netflix on their platforms.

# Our View On The Stock

# An Unquestionably Strong Growth Story, But A Valuation That Is Ahead Of The Fundamentals

# Netflix's Strategy Is To Aggressively Reinvest in Content To Drive Subscriber Growth And Achieve A Sustainable Leadership Position In The Market

We think that Netflix will need to continue to invest aggressively in content in order to sustain its desired growth trajectory, achieve a global position of scale, and meet the unique challenges posed by the company's international expansion. We believe that this means the timing and scale of profitability is further away than many believe. Consensus profit estimates, in our view, are too high by a significant degree. Our EBIT estimates are  $27\%$  lower than the consensus mean for 2017-2020, on a cumulative basis, and  $23\%$  lower in 2020.

# While Netflix Has Already Become A Force In Hollywood, It Does Not Yet Represent An Outsized Percentage Of Television Programming Costs

Based on research from John Landgraf's team at FX Networks, 2016 is on pace for about 440 original scripted series in 2016, up  $5\%$  from what he previously called "Peak TV" in 2015. Netflix's 31 scripted originals, which exclude Kids content, represents  $7.5\%$  of total production. Based on content spend by the companies under our coverage, we estimate that the industry aggregators spend approximately  $46\%$  of revenues on programming. Applying that figure to our industry estimate for advertising and affiliate revenue, and backing out sports rights costs, we estimate Netflix's  $\$2.4B$  in domestic content spend represents slightly over  $6\%$  industry television programming costs. If we include the contribution from other SVOD players (most notably Amazon, Hulk & YouTube), we estimate Netflix represents just over  $5\%$  of industry spend on content.

# We See Our Programming Expense Forecast As Reasonable, Rather Than Conservative

Management has bold ambitions for both global expansion and continued growth in the US. While we see the favorable US dynamics as already in place and well understood by the market, we think the international opportunity is a very long game and will require more investment than the market is anticipating. We also think the rate of international subscriber growth might be slower than the market is expecting given that Netflix has only begun to invest in most markets. In short, we see challenges in the international segment that could take longer and cost more to overcome than the market is anticipating. We discuss this in more detail in the International section of this report. Lastly, achieving management's 60-90M long term domestic subscriber target will require continued growth in programming costs, which we think the market might also be underestimating. Continued programming investment is the key to growing the subscriber base and driving pricing power.

# Our Forecast Is Consistent With Some Of Management's Commentary On The Multi-Year Programming Expense Outlook

Management has articulated a long term target of a 50/50 mix between licensed and original content, but has indicated that even the licensed portion will grow. Our forecast achieves this approximately 50/50 mix in 2018, with all of the worldwide licensing expense growth in the International segment in order to drive growth. US licensing remains flat through 2020, with all US programming expense growth driven by originals. Originals also grow within International; we allocate  $57\%$  of all global original content to International and  $43\%$  to domestic.

Management commented in early 2016 that it wants to increase average subscriber engagements with originals from once per month to once per week. Looking at this through the lens of simple math, it implies that management intends to increase original programming by four times in the US from 2015. This is also embedded in our US forecast. Applying the US vs international allocation methodology (we assume  $43\%$  US) then yields the international portion of global originals expense.

# We Expect Decreasing Returns On Incremental Programming Expense

Our forecast assumes that every incremental dollar of programming expense yields less subscriber growth now that penetration in the US exceeds  $50\%$  and the initial cream-skimming in many international markets will be over soon. Looking at programming expense on a per subscriber per month basis, we forecast the US to go from  $3.87 in 2015 to$ 4.84 in 2020, a  $4.6\%$  CAGR. It seems logical to us that the incremental domestic subscriber will be more difficult to attract/retain as compared to the first 45M and therefore, programming expense per subscriber will trend higher. On the international side, we view Netflix's content investment and current global push to result in per subscriber growth this year, but subscriber growth will reverse the unit costs, modestly over the next several years. We see per subscriber programming costs declining to \$5.44 in 2020 (down  $1.1\%$  annually from 2015's \$5.75).

# We Estimate The Stock Price Is 2 Years Ahead of the Fundamentals

We arrive at our 12-month PT by using our market based DCF model, which looks at the returns to equity implied by our forecast based on the company's current valuation. We estimate that based on our forecast, at the current stock price, Netflix will return a  $10\%$  compounded annual rate of return to equity holders over the next 10 years. We think investors' required rate of return on Netflix is higher than  $10\%$ . We assume a  $12.5\%$  cost of equity.

We believe the major point of differentiation between our valuation for Netflix and the market's is the company's medium to long term margin structure. Sell-side consensus estimates imply a P/E multiple of about 23x 2020 EPS based on today's stock price, while our earnings forecast implies 31x 2020 EPS. Our model assumes that Netflix will turn FCF positive in 2020, while consensus appears to assume the company will generate positive FCF in 2018.

# An Acquisition By Disney or Apple Is Unlikely

In our opinion, Netflix's business model is too fully formed and well understood by the market to be acquired by Disney or Apple, as mentioned in recent press articles (Barron's). Acquisition targets for these companies, ideally, are those that bring unique capabilities, IP, or technology that are more valuable when leveraged by these bigger companies' capabilities in their core businesses. Companies with unique technology, IP, or other capabilities, but without strong business models, are ideal targets because of the financial upside that is not reflected in their standalone market valuations. However, Netflix has a fully formed business model that is richly valued in the market, making it very hard for a strategic acquirer to acquire Netflix and earn its cost of capital on the purchase. It seems to us that if Disney or Apple acquired Netflix and used it for the betterment of its core business, then it would be value destructive to Netflix's core business value, requiring the acquirer to

create more value somewhere else than is destroyed in Netflix's core business, a very tall order. Furthermore, the economic and earnings dilution would be severe in the case of Disney.

# Apple

In order for Apple (covered by DB Analyst Sherri Scribner) to create value in its core business from Netflix, Apple would have to degrade the Netflix experience (or fail to innovate the experience) on all non-Apple hardware/software platforms, something that would clearly diminish Netflix's core business value as a hardware neutral service. This makes structuring a economically accretive deal challenging for Apple. If Apple were to acquire Netflix, it would be the company's largest acquisition ever.

# A Deal Would Be Very Dilutive For Disney Shareholders (25% EPS Dilution)

Netflix would, naturally, provide a new source of earnings growth for Disney, but with Netflix trading north of 100x 2018 earnings, the dilution would be  $\sim 25\%$  even through F2021. Culturally, we also find it hard to see how Netflix could be successfully integrated into Disney. Our analysis, which assumes a  \$130 per share M&A take out, 50/50 cash/equity and an incremental\$ 200M in run-rate corporate cost synergies, results in a pro forma DIS EPS  $25\%$  below our stand-alone DIS estimates. The deal would also leave DIS levered at 2.5x Net Debt / PF '17 EBITDA, significantly higher than the sub 1x we see today.

The Defensive Angle Doesn't Make Sense For Disney, Non-ESPN TV Operating Income Is Only  $\sim 17\%$  of the DIS Total.

Disney's core TV challenge is ESPN's maturity - declining subscribers, a high wholesale price point, and (to a lesser extent) Sports Center's format becoming less relevant in the digital age. We don't see how Netflix would resolve any of these challenges as a non-sports, ad-free service that sits outside of the pay TV ecosystem. Disney couldn't realistically marry ESPN with Netflix's service - it would make Netflix too expensive, driving high churn because an estimated  $85\%$  of Netflix subscribers already have ESPN through their pay TV subscription. Consumers certainly won't pay for ESPN twice, they would just drop their Netflix subscriptions; not their pay TV subscriptions because they need all the other networks on pay TV that carry sports if they are indeed sports fans. If they are not sports fans, then they won't pay more for Netflix because ESPN has been added, causing them to churn.

The arguments for synergies between Disney and Netflix are not compelling, in our view.

1. There's no meaningful synergies with Disney's production business. The studio already makes films and licenses the TV window for Disney films to Netflix for a few hundred million dollars per year (only about  $5\%$  of Netflix's programming budget in 2017). The monetization would be the same if Disney went to other TV/SVOD outlets, which demonstrates how little value exists within that window for movies.  
2. On the TV production side, Disney already produces shows for and licenses content to Netflix. The studio is not having a problem selling its shows into a robust competitive market that includes traditional networks, as well as Netflix, Amazon, and Hulu. Nor is Netflix having a problem sourcing shows from studios, including Disney. TV production is sufficiently fragmented that there are ample buyers for Netflix to utilize. In fact, the only way for Netflix to continue growing is to source content from many studios because maintaining the diversity of programming is essential to building on what is already a very high penetration level; it is also necessary to increase pricing power. Netflix actually has more content on its balance sheet right now than Disney, and it is growing. Due to the fragmentation of production, there's little in the way of cost synergies because studios deal with their vertically integrated programming assets (whether it is Netflix, a traditional network, or Hulu) on an arm's length basis, licensing shows to the highest bidder. Netflix is licensing shows from everyone. In addition, Netflix is building its own production studio inside of Netflix. Netflix is already a first stop for most new projects; thus Disney would only enhance Netflix's access to content marginally.  
3. Other cost synergies would have to be balanced against maintaining the culture of innovation, focus, and continuous improvement that exists at Netflix today. We believe that Netflix, if acquired by Disney, would have to be run as an autonomous business unit in order to preserve these vital cultural elements that have driven the company's success to date. The only area where there would be any cost synergies, in our estimation, would be in G&A. Netflix's G&A, in total, we estimate is about \$550M in 2016. We think the G&A cost synergies would be limited to maybe \$100M or so given the need to run Netflix autonomously.

# The Offensive Argument For A Disney Deal

The only reason we can see for Disney seriously considering acquiring Netflix is the international growth opportunity. To be clear, however, this an opportunity that Netflix is already executing on and is reflected in the stock price. Disney has dabbled in international OTT with its Disney Life service (UK, China), but otherwise is not pursuing anything at scale in international OTT. Disney could be interested in Netflix because it sees an even bigger international opportunity than is priced into Netflix's shares today. Disney has extensive experience operating internationally, which could benefit Netflix's global growth. All of that said, the strategic rationale for putting Disney and Netflix together is not all that compelling.

We see Netflix as a subscription-based, direct-to-consumer programmer, a company more similar to other content aggregators and distributors in the traditional media sector. However, we recognize that many investors like to put Netflix in the same category as Amazon, Facebook, and Google; completing the FANG acronym. Therefore, we provide our perspective here on the relative comparison of the other FANG stocks to Netflix. Ross Sandler covers FB, AMZN, and GOOG for Deutsche Bank.

The exhibit below compares valuation multiples and relative performance across the FANG stocks. In addition to the other FANGs having more near to medium term valuation support, we also believe there are fundamental differences in their respective global opportunities, and differences in their option value related to value creation in non-core, but related businesses (e.g. AWS and shipping for Amazon, autonomous vehicles for Google).

The market's bull case for Netflix is that it can become to Internet-delivered TV what Google is to Search and short-form video, and what Facebook is to Social Media. We've also heard investors frame Netflix as analogue to Amazon; a multi-decade growth story that can funnel cash flow back into the business, delaying profitability all while expanding its TAM (i.e. an envelop strategy). While we do think this is consistent with management's long-term plan, we see them as structurally different opportunities. Google and Facebook are better businesses. They are open platforms with scaled user bases and, therefore, significant network effects and no content costs. This makes them natural monopolies in many ways. Their services are free to end-users and can be used even in very low bandwidth environments, resulting in a larger TAM and much higher natural penetration levels. Both also benefit from virtuous cycles whereby the more people use the service, the better the service becomes for others. Netflix's addressable market, International broadband households, is only at 590M, projected to increase to 800M by 2020 (including China, in which entry is highly uncertain) while Google's and Facebook's addressable market, the global mobile phone user base, is estimated at 3.8B, increasing to 4.6B by 2020. Furthermore, because Google and Facebook don't actually provide the content on their platforms, they are not limited by the need to invest in local content in order to drive penetrations deeper, whereas Netflix will have this constraint. Netflix's global pricing strategy is also limiting given income levels and propensity to spend on content, as well as other factors in many markets, which we discuss in more detail in the International section.

Amazon's opportunity, setting aside its AWS business and international retail operations, is  \$4.8T in the US (essentially the entire retail industry). In contrast, we estimate the entire US Pay-TV industry generates\$ 100B in subscription revenue annually. Amazon's core retail business also has much more fragmentation in the supplier base. While there is fragmentation in the studios and talent that ultimately supply Netflix content, the major studios and media conglomerates play a major role and compete with Netflix.

The scale in technology research & development is also a differentiator. Google, Amazon, and Facebook employee large armies of engineers working on a wide range of technologies and applications. They are also touching massive numbers of consumers and processing massive amounts of information. This scale naturally leads to new opportunities in areas that can enhance the core business, or create new standalone opportunities that don't necessarily relate to the core.

At the end of the day, we see comparisons to Google, Facebook or Amazon as largely irrelevant in thinking about Netflix.

# Competition: Netflix Is Not The First Mover In Many International Markets

Netflix will surely benefit from its experience in the US and earlier launched international markets. However, it has recently entered many markets where there is already one or more earlier entrants, most of which are backed by local TV incumbents with existing US content rights and their own original programming. Below are some examples of large markets with competitive services that Netflix is or will be competing against.

Germany: Maxdome is an 8/month advertising-free SVOD service with a catalog equivalent in size to Netflix. Maxdome comes pre-installed on TVs sold in Germany and the service offers Hollywood TV series and movies, as well as exclusive ProSieben free-to-air (FTA) content. Maxdome is owned by

ProSieben, one of the large TV incumbents. Amazon Prime, Watchever (owned by Vivendi), and Sky Online are also SVOD services operating in Germany.

India: EROS Now and Hot Star compete in India. EROS Now is owned by Eros, the largest Bollywood studio, which represents about 40% of the Indian box office. Eros Now is priced at ~\$0.75/month for basic and ~\$1.50 for the premium tier. EROS Now offers movies, TV, original series, and music. EROS Now also offers the capability to download content to one's device, which is particularly important in a country with an immature broadband infrastructure. Eros recently announced that the service passed 49M registered users, but only 1.1M actively paying subscribers even at a low price point and with content that has broad appeal in India. Another OTT service is Hotstar, which is owned by Star India, which is owned by Fox. Hotstar offers TV series, movies, and sports; and it is free. Pay TV ARPU in India averages \$2.50 per month, thus, Netflix is not price competitive with Pay-TV there. HBO, while it does not offer a direct-to-consumer service in India, earns about a \$0.14 ARPU in India.

China: The list of challenges associated with entering China is long, including that there are many competitors with substantial head starts, namely Tmall Box Office ("TBO", owned by Alibaba), whose model closely resembles Netflix's. In addition, there's Youku Tudou (owned by Alibaba), PPTV, iQiyi, PPS.tv, Sohu Video, LeTV, Tencent TV, M1905.com, Sina Video, CBox, and Funshion. China also requires SVOD services to have  $70\%$  of their content sourced from China. We think Netflix would need a partner to enter China successfully.

Other Europe: Canal Plus has launched Canal Play in France, Mediaset has launched Mediaset Premium in Italy, Sky offers TV Choice in the UK, and Amazon Prime operates in all of these markets.

# Piracy Levels Also Vary

Piracy is another factor in willingness to pay for content. Every market has piracy, some more than others. We observe that piracy is highest where there isn't affordable legal access to the content being sought. Therefore, the logic follows that in markets with high standards of living (e.g. Australia), piracy will decrease noticeably as the availability of legal content increases. However, in markets with low income levels (e.g. India) or significant government censorship (e.g. China), piracy rates are likely to be less impacted by Netflix

entry. Broadband access is also a factor, since one cannot download or file-share without access to the internet and adequate bandwidth. There probably is also a habit factor, as well. Those who have always pirated content such that it has become second nature for them will be less inclined to start paying for content. Lastly, the legal enforcement of IP protection also has to be a factor. Russia, China, Eastern Europe, parts of Latin America, and Southern Europe are generally viewed as the areas where piracy rates are highest.

# Regulatory Hurdles Exist, More Could Materialize

Media regulation, censorship, fee collection, and ownership concerns are fixtures of the global media landscape. Netflix flipped the switch to make Netflix's service available in 130 new countries in January 2016, including many of the least friendly regimes to foreign media organizations. China, for example, (which Netflix has not yet entered) has strict censorship rules and requires that at least  $70\%$  of an SVOD service's content is Chinese content. Issues could come up in the future if governments (or their constituents) become concerned that Netflix is hurting their domestic media industry or pushing the limits on censorship.

# Payment Infrastructure

Netflix's US model is based on credit card (or PayPal) billing. When the company initially began its international expansion, the natural question was how would countries with low credit card penetration pay? This is initially a limiting factor whenever Netflix enters a market with low credit card penetration. However, Netflix has managed through this by developing alternative methods of payment. For example, in Brazil, customers can pay with prepaid Visa and MasterCard cards, direct debit from bank accounts, Netflix Gift Cards, and iTunes, in addition to PayPal and credit cards. Debit cards, iTunes, and Netflix gift cards can also be used for payment in the US.

# Device Penetration

A barrier to subscription exists for households that do not have a Smart TV, OTT device, tablet, PC, smart phone, or STB UI that offers Netflix. In general, penetrations for these devices are lower than the US in most markets, but all are growing.

# Virtual Private Networks (VPNs):

In mid-January 2016 David Fullagar, VP of Content Delivery Architecture at Netflix, wrote a press release describing Netflix's use of proxy detection to deter subscribers from streaming content not available to them in their home markets. Content owners who sell content into specific markets clearly want Netflix to enforce, to the extent it can, geographic licensing boundaries. Prior to Netflix tightening its VPN enforcement tactics, it was well established that some users in certain international geographies were using VPNs to appear as if they were US subscribers to Netflix's servers. Our checks indicate that Netflix has beefed up its efforts in detecting users use of such VPNs. The implications of a more effective ban on VPNs are unclear. On one hand, we think this could present a near term headwind to international subscriber growth as some international subscribers, conditioned to having access to the full US content line-up, view the local version in a less favorable light. On the other hand, if Netflix truly has the ability to monitor the usage, then it perhaps enables a much more flexible global pricing strategy. One reason, among others, why Netflix has rolled out a fairly uniform global pricing structure, is that the use of VPNs could enable tech-savvy subscribers the ability to price arbitrage the service.

# ARPU Growth Outlook

# Growth In Usage and Subscribers Supports Pricing Power, with Further ARPU Benefit From 4K Mix Shift

Consumption of Netflix continues to grow nicely on a per subscriber basis. The company reported more than 40B hours of content streamed globally at its 2016 CES event, implying per subscriber usage trending towards 1.8 hours per day. We note that this is a global number, but the company has mentioned

usage rates don't dramatically differ across geographies. As we discuss later in this report, the per-subscriber usage needs some adjustment if we compare to traditional TV because of password sharing. In short, we believe that a single Netflix subscriber encompasses more than 1 traditional Pay-TV subscription. Nonetheless, as Netflix users find more and more value in the service, we think that portends healthy opportunity for pricing power. The further adoption of 4K TVs also presents a mix shift-related pricing opportunity that could aid in growing ARPU for Netflix.

On a cost per hour of usage, Netflix prices at a significant discount to traditional TV. Of course, Netflix offers no sports, no news, and most of the content still represents repeats that have already been on TV. Repeats are inherently less valuable to a consumer than first run programming. Nielsen estimates that the average American household watches more than 8 hours of traditional TV per day, of which about  $70\%$  is live and  $30\%$  is time-shifted. We also backed out an estimate of advertising time, roughly a 15min average ad-load. We also assume that in non-linear viewing,  $60\%$  of what would have been ad consumption is skipped. The conclusion here is that Netflix offers good value.

Note that using mean usage is probably misleading as heavy streamers in all likelihood are pulling the mean up to a level that is noticeably higher than the median usage. We see this with broadband usage in general. Netflix cannot make pricing decisions based on mean usage, and it certainly doesn't, nor can we judge its pricing power based on mean usage.

# Programming Expense Outlook: How Much Operating Leverage Is There In Programming Expense?

# Willingness To Pay Seemingly Constrained Amongst Owners

Our survey indicated that account owners would be willing to spend, on average, up to \$17 per month on their Netflix subscription; a healthy increase from the effective ARPU today of ~\$9 per month. # Netflix Is Rarely An Exclusive Video Source For Users; It Is Complementary

Netflix has long maintained its place in the entertainment budget for consumers as complementary - to traditional Pay-TV and other streaming services. Our survey confirms that view. Only  $5\%$  of our survey (which required Netflix usage to participate) used Netflix exclusively amongst either traditional Pay-TV or other streaming services. Pay-TV exposure among account owners in our survey was  $84\%$ . Among Shareers and Co-Op Shareers, we saw lower levels of Pay-TV penetration, but still fairly robust in the  $70 - 75\%$  range. Moreover, there's also a strong indication that consumers are using multiple streaming services, with half of our sample subscribing to Amazon Prime and Hulu. Nearly  $1/3$  of our sample reported using Netflix, Amazon and Hulu.

Therefore, we don't think that consumers do, nor investors should, view Netflix's pricing power as a function of replacing or substituting Pay-TV. Rather, we see evidence to suggest that Netflix will compete with others for share of wallet, which isn't a zero-sum game, but it isn't the fertile delta between Pay-TV and Netflix, either.

# Competitive Dynamics In The US

# Is Access to Content Becoming A Problem For Netflix?

We think that maintaining Netflix's current level of domestic licensing spend is achievable despite more competition from Hulu and Amazon, as well as increasing retention by the networks of full season stacking rights for current seasons of their shows. The reason we believe this to be the case is threefold. First, the rights to past seasons of shows are still readily available to Netflix. Second, the amount of TV shows being made has been increasing for the past  $5+$  years (and is still increasing), meaning that the number of shows hitting syndication/licensing windows every year is still on the rise and will continue to grow for at least another few years. Third, the broadcast networks are being more selective than ever in procuring programming from third party studios so that they can monetize the back end of their shows, which results in more product available to Netflix from the likes of Warner Brothers, Sony, and smaller independent studios. This last point applies to both licensed shows and Netflix originals.

Participation rights and financing structures obligate studios to sell to the highest bidder, which is one of the key reasons the industry needed to fund Hulu. A studio simply saying no to Netflix's offer to license a show was not an option given these obligations, thus Hulu provides a competitive bidder for licensed content in the same windows as Netflix. Over time, contractual terms can adapt to give the vertically integrated studios more flexibility in directing where and to whom content is sold. We doubt these studios are now interested in taking on more financial and creative risk in order to starve Netflix of content, but in theory, studios could get more creative with how they structure participations in order to make it more difficult for Netflix to access content. However, doing so could impair a studio's competitiveness as a magnet for talent.

While making a show for Netflix is, today, considered an exciting proposition by many in the business of making television shows, the disadvantage for talent is that any original program made for Netflix only lives on Netflix. There's no participation in a back end with huge upside potential if a show becomes a hit. One could argue that the talent can negotiate for better for terms after a few seasons if a show turns out to be a hit, but the questions is will they even know if it is a hit and, if so, to what degree? Unlike the traditional television world, there would seem to be a lack of price discovery for talent with Netflix. This information asymmetry is a benefit to Netflix over the long run, assuming it doesn't impair the company's competitiveness in attracting talent and top new shows. Netflix can, naturally, share more information with talent if it needs to; this is just part of the negotiation.

A recent blog article by Exstreamist, an SVOD focused media blog, suggests that the overall quantity of titles available to US subscribers is down on the order of  $50\%$  since 2012. We think Netflix has culled the library of licensed content by design, increasing the amount of original content and allocating

more dollars toward fewer licensed titles to improve quality while reducing clutter. Our intuition is that to date, this has been a function of more competitive bidding from Amazon and Hulu and a strategic decision by Netflix rather than a concerted effort from sellers to keep content away from Netflix.

# Who Spends What on Content

We looked at implied valuations in the context of Enterprise Value to annual content amortization expense for some of the major media companies and Netflix. The companies more exposed to the general entertainment cable networks business, given their affiliate revenue generation, trade between 6x and 7x, a premium to FOX and CBS at  $\sim 5\mathrm{x}$ ; which have greater exposure to broadcast. The broadcast networks and station groups are still developing their retransmission revenue reverse compensation streams, thus, their monetization is improving and narrowing the gap with cable networks over time. DISCA and SNI trade at 8-10x due to their lower cost programming and resulting margins. Netflix trades at more than 8x our projected 2016 content spend. Netflix is clearly growing content spend more aggressively than the comparable set, but we think this indicates that there are lofty profit growth expectations embedded in Netflix's current valuation.

The natural counterpoint to this analysis is that if one believes Netflix's margins will be higher than traditional TV (i.e. monetization is superior) and Netflix's growth rate is higher, then why shouldn't the company trade at a higher EV/programming expense multiple? In our view, it is unlikely that Netflix will end up with higher margins than the cable networks have today given increasing competition in the programming (i.e. content aggregation) level of the industry (where Netflix primarily sits), increasing competition in the distribution layer of the industry, and the fact that Netflix, as a direct-to-consumer service, doesn't benefit from the pay TV bundle oligopoly. Furthermore, there will be a significant amount of FCF generated by the traditional media companies, which will reduce EV in the future (all else equal) through debt repayment and share repurchases.

# Is The Unbundling of Networks: Is It Good Or Bad For Netflix?

The unbundling of networks creates more competition; fragmentation that potentially makes it harder for Netflix to stand out. However, with Netflix stepping up its original programming spend meaningfully while increasing licensed programming spend at the same time, Netflix's strong value proposition is highlighted against competitors with thinner content offerings. We think Netflix will always be a complementary service given the continued importance of live programming, but fragmentation of the big bundle could provide an opportunity for Netflix to play a larger role in the construction of smaller bundles, either formally or DIY.

# Hulu Stepping Up Its Game

Ever since Hulu owners 21st Century Fox, Disney, and Comcast agreed to infuse \(750M in equity capital into the company in 2013, Hulu has become a much stronger competitor in the OTT space, both with current season broadcast shows and a growing library of prior season licensed content and even a few originals. Furthermore, Hulu has gone out and secured high profile content like the entire library of Seinfeld, South Park, and broader deals with Viacom, Epix, and other cable networks. We think Hulu has significantly narrowed the gap with Netflix in regards to its UI and platform availability. That said, Hulu hasn't yet proven that it can narrow the subscriber gap with Netflix.

Many in the industry thought Hulu could help to preserve the TV advertising ecosystem and capture younger demos. Somewhat contrary to that thesis, Hulu introduced an ad-free option for \$11.99 in September 2015, vs. the \$5.99 ad-supported price point. We initially scratched our head at the idea that its media conglomerate owners would be interested in potentially accelerating the shift towards non ad-supported models, but in the context of competing with Netflix we think it was ultimately a needed strategic move. We estimate Hulu is now approaching 12M subs, still well behind Netflix, but certainly moving in the right direction.

# Hulu Live & DIRECTV Now

Hulu Live, a  $40-$ 50 (introductory tier) per month service is targeted to launch in early 2017. This will essentially be a pay TV service that is streamed over the internet. DIRECTV is planning to launch a similar service by YE16. Sling TV and Sony Vue represent similar services that are already in the market. Hulu Live has the luxury of under-pricing traditional MVPD price points as it can price the content essentially at cost, removing the distributor costs and margin in the process. In addition, the consumer will bear most of the video transport cost through her home broadband connection, a cost that consumers largely think of as fixed. The lower price point should lead to a higher pay TV penetration than we'd otherwise see, which we think could result in a flat to positive growth outlook for pay TV subscribers. So long as Hulu owners are receiving similar or better affiliate fees for their content, they should be relatively agnostic if they monetize through Hulu or the traditional ecosystem.

We don't see Hulu Live or DIRECTV Now as major threats to Netflix because they are just Pay-TV services, and the vast majority of Netflix subscribers already have Pay-TV. However, we do think that they will convert some number of households (in the millions) from non Pay-TV to Pay-TV households. This should reduce the utility of Netflix among this group of households, which in theory should reduce pricing power to a degree. However, we do not see Hulu Live and DIRECTV Now as a driver if disconnects or lower gross adds for Netflix.

# Could Rebundling Be A Threat?

We know that the majority of Netflix subscribers also subscribe to Pay-TV and some subscribe to Hulu, Amazon Prime, or both. We know that Apple TV, Roku, and other popular devices are increasingly integrating services with features like universal search that can discover content across multiple services. To us, this seems to indicate that Netflix is likely to remain a complementary service, at least domestically. We think this creates a ceiling with respect to pricing power for the service. We're not discounting the value proposition of Netflix, but with respect to the platform vs. network/aggregator question we think the trend supports the latter.

On the other hand, we think Netflix could see some benefit from traditional distributors incorporating Netflix into MVPD set top boxes. DISH has integrated Netflix into its Hopper series boxes for some time now. Cable STBs with Tivo UIs have integrated Netflix for some time. The coup de grace, however, came recently with the announcements that Liberty Global and Comcast would be integrating Netflix into their platforms. Comcast has announced a Fall '16 update to its X1 platform that will incorporate Netflix. We think Charter seems willing to do something similar.

# Usage Based Pricing Is Bad For Netflix

Usage based pricing is standard practice today in the mobile industry. Its use is less consistent, but still common, in the fixed line broadband business. For large operators, it remains a political lightning rod, which is the reason we've seen data allowances move higher (Comcast has gone from 250 GB initially to now 1 TB). Netflix recently asked the FCC to consider regulating the use of usage-based pricing for broadband. We believe that some operators (e.g. Charter, Verizon, Altice) will continue to offer an unlimited broadband tier in accordance with their business plan. Comcast's 1 TB allowance still leaves significant breathing room for nearly all customers. However, we think that caps in the 300 GB range will increasingly be exceeded; triggering overage

charges, potentially raising political/regulatory concerns, and negatively impacting Netflix's business. In a worst case scenario, usage-based pricing could mean that Netflix needs to have a smaller content catalog in the future because consumers will limit their consumption.

# Amazon: Prime Instant Video

Amazon Prime Instant Video has the clear advantage in terms of being bundled into a suite of services in which customers find value. We think most subscribers find the Free Two-Day Shipping the major draw, but with Prime Video, Prime Music, Prime Reading, unlimited photo storage in Amazon Cloud (Prime Photos), there's many sources of consumer surplus. At \(99 per year (\)8.25 /mo), Prime Membership, for many, is a great deal. Amazon can do this because Prime enhances the competitive position of Amazon's e-commerce business through customer captivity and habit formation. DB's Ross Sandler and team laid out the case for Prime in their July 2014 piece "Going Prime-Time Extends the Competitive Moat". Amazon continues to extend the value proposition of Prime Membership: Prime Now (1-hour delivery in select markets), Prime Fresh (unlimited grocery delivery, \)200 upcharge annually), etc. We see no reason to believe that Amazon will show reticence to continue adding benefits and improving their offering. Furthermore, Amazon has a global footprint and we expect that international customers are likely to find Prime membership compelling, as well. Ross Sandler estimates Prime memberships now total 75M+ globally, in-line with Netflix's global streaming membership tally. In terms of its ability to scale globally, acquire content, and establish brand-equity with consumers, we think Amazon could be a formidable competitor to Netflix. Recently, Jeff Bezos framed the opportunity for Prime Video succinctly, "we get to monetize (Prime Video) in an unusual waywhen we win a Golden Globe, we sell more shoes". Being able to monetize in alternative ways is a major advantage.

In mid-December 2015, Amazon announced its "Streaming Partners Program" where Prime members can expand content choices through add-ons; notably Showtime and Starz for \$8.99/mo each. The unbundling of traditional networks is well under-way, but now we are starting to see re-bundling. Amazon's Prime platform should be a very attractive platform for content owners and networks as it is large, growing and global. Most recently, Amazon announced standalone monthly Prime Video pricing for \$8.99 per month (as well as the full Prime bundle for \$10.99 per month).

We think Amazon has been slow to fully embrace Android and iOS (or tvOS, for that matter) given that Amazon is selling hardware that is meant to compete with Apple TV and Chromecast. Amazon has developed applications for both Android and iOS, but has yet to roll out on Apple's new operating system (tvOS). Amazon conspicuously removed both the Apple TV and Chromecast from amazon.com, but we doubt that Amazon will choose to remain off of those operating system platforms in the long term. Amazon Prime Instant Video is available on the major consoles, mobile platforms, smart TVs, and Roku. Given Apple TV's scale and now open platform, we expect Amazon to develop a tvOS app.

Furthermore, we see a live OTT video component as something that makes strategic sense for Amazon, given their unique ability to monetize through retail sales. At the very least, we know Amazon takes a very long view and is certainly not afraid to be very disruptive.

# Amazon Studios: Sourcing and Developing Original Content

Amazon launched Amazon Studios in late 2010 as a way to essentially crowd-source TV and film scripts and concepts. Amazon provides free screen-writing software to help facilitate the process. An article from Wired in 2013 suggested that to that point, Amazon had received nearly 2,700 television scripts and now accepts scripts from members of the Writers Guild of America. Clearly, only a very small percent of ideas submitted are taken to pilot and ultimately become series, but Amazon's development pipeline in original content is not insignificant. According to FutonCritic.com, Amazon has  $\sim 14$  original series in development that have ordered to series and should appear on the service in the near term. SVOD operators have the luxury of not bearing opportunity cost related to linear schedules (there's only one Sunday 9PM EST), but Amazon seems to be building Prime Video with intentions of significant scale.

We estimate that Amazon has released about 255 hours of original content to date, with a strong pipeline of new shows yet to come. Both Transparent and Mozart In The Jungle, have won major Golden Globe or Emmy categories. Man In The High Castle, Bosch, Catastrophe, Red Oaks, and others have also garnered positive critical reviews. With Amazon's unique monetization strategy and hyper-focus on the consumer experience, we think Amazon will be a force in video.

Netflix is not the only programmer pursuing a significant push into expanded scripted content. As we mentioned previously, the industry has significantly ramped content production over the last handful of years. Still, Netflix alone represents about  $7\%$  of current US production, albeit growing faster than the industry.
